Mexican Holidays

A celebration of our traditions and culture

Day of the Dead

The Day of the Dead, celebrated on November 1 and 2, is one of the most significant and colorful traditions in Mexico. Its origins date back to pre-Hispanic times, when the Mexica, Maya, Purépecha and Totonac cultures already practiced rituals to honor the deceased. During this celebration, it is believed that the souls of loved ones return to the world of the living to live with their families and enjoy the offerings prepared especially for them.

Grito de Independencia

September 15 and 16 - The Grito de Independencia is the symbol of the birth of a free and sovereign nation, and every year is a date of pride and reflection on the sacrifice and bravery of those who fought for Mexico's independence.

Day of the Virgin of Guadalupe

December 12 - The Day of the Virgin of Guadalupe is not only a religious date, but also has a strong cultural and social component. It is a manifestation of the popular Mexican faith, united by a common feeling of devotion and gratitude to the Virgin. Throughout the centuries, the figure of the Virgin has been a symbol of protection, hope and unity.

Christmas in Mexico

December 25 - Christmas in Mexico is a time full of traditions and family celebrations. Nativity scenes representing the birth of Jesus are placed in homes, and families gather to share Christmas dinner. Typical dishes include tamales, cod, and ponche. Posadas, which begin on December 16, recreate the journey of Mary and Joseph in search of lodging before the birth of Jesus. These include songs, piñatas, and lights, symbolizing unity and hope during the Christmas season.

Mexican Revolution

November 20 - The Mexican Revolution marked a key historical moment for Mexico. Begun in 1910, this struggle sought to end more than 30 years of dictatorship by Porfirio Díaz and to confront the deep social and economic inequalities of the time. Important figures such as Francisco I. Madero, Emiliano Zapata and Pancho Villa played a fundamental role in the movement. The revolution brought about significant changes, including a new constitution in 1917 that established labor, agrarian and educational rights, and sought to guarantee social justice for the Mexican people.
